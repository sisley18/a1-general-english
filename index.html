Meeting People in New York

📖 American Culture Notes

New York is a big city. People are always busy. When Americans meet for the first time, they shake hands and say, 'Nice to meet you!' or 'How are you doing?' They use first names quickly.

🗣️ Everyday Dialogue

Sarah: Hi, I'm Sarah. Nice to meet you!

Mike: Nice to meet you too, Sarah. I'm Mike. Where are you from?

Sarah: I'm from Boston. How about you?

Mike: I'm from Los Angeles. Welcome to New York!

📚 Vocabulary

Essential Vocabulary

Greetings

Hello / Hi
A common greeting
"Hello! How are you?"
Good morning
A greeting used before 12 PM
"Good morning, teacher!"
Good afternoon
A greeting used after 12 PM
"Good afternoon, class."
Good evening
A greeting used in the evening
"Good evening, Mr. Smith."
Goodbye / Bye
Said when leaving
"Goodbye! See you tomorrow."
Nice to meet you
Said when you meet someone for the first time
"I am Anna. Nice to meet you."

Personal Information

Name
What people call you
"My name is Carlos."
Country
Where you are from
"I am from Argentina."
Age
How old you are
"I am twenty-five years old."
Job / Occupation
What you do for work
"I am a student."
📝 Grammar

Verb "To Be" — Present Simple

I am → I'm a teacher.
You are → You're my friend.
He is / She is / It is → She's from Brazil.
We are → We're students.
They are → They're happy.
Questions: Are you a student? Is she from Mexico?
Negatives: I am not (I'm not) tired. He is not (isn't) a doctor.
💫 Key Collocations

Useful Expressions for Introductions

"Where are you from? I'm from Spain."
How are you?

"How are you? I'm fine, thank you."
What do you do?

"What do you do? I'm a nurse."

American Families

📖 American Culture Notes

In the United States, families are diverse. Many people live with their parents and siblings in a house or an apartment. Extended families (grandparents, aunts, uncles) often get together for Thanksgiving in November.

🗣️ Everyday Dialogue

Lisa: Do you have a big family?

Tom: No, I have a small family. Just my mom, dad, and my younger sister.

Lisa: Do your grandparents live with you?

Tom: No, they live in Florida. We visit them every summer.

A Typical American Morning

📖 American Culture Notes

Many Americans wake up early, around 6:30 AM. They take a quick shower, drink a cup of coffee, and eat cereal or toast. Then, they take the bus or drive their car to work or school.

🗣️ Everyday Dialogue

Emma: What time do you usually get up?

Jake: I get up at 7:00 AM. I grab a coffee and go to work.

Emma: Do you eat breakfast?

Jake: Sometimes. Usually just a donut or a bagel.

1. 📚 Vocabulary

Daily Activities

Wake up
To stop sleeping
"I wake up at seven o'clock."
Have breakfast
To eat the first meal of the day
"I have breakfast at half past seven."
Go to work / school
To travel to your job or school
"I go to work by bus."
Have lunch
To eat the midday meal
"I have lunch at twelve thirty."
Come home
To return to your house
"I come home at five o'clock."
Have dinner
To eat the evening meal
"We have dinner at eight o'clock."
Take a shower
To wash your body with water
"I take a shower every morning."
Go to bed
To go to sleep
"I go to bed at ten thirty."

Visual Grammar: Telling the Time

  • ... o'clock (Minute 00)
    "It is three o'clock" (3:00)
  • Quarter past ... (Minute 15)
    "It is quarter past three" (3:15)
  • Half past ... (Minute 30)
    "It is half past three" (3:30)
  • Quarter to ... (Minute 45)
    "It is quarter to four" (3:45)
2. 📝 Grammar

Present Simple — Daily Routines

I / You / We / They → I get up at seven. They eat lunch at noon.
He / She / It → She gets up at six. He eats lunch at one.
Negatives: I don't work on Sundays. She doesn't drive to work.
Questions: Do you work on Saturdays? Does he take the bus?

Eating Out at a Diner

📖 American Culture Notes

An American diner is a casual restaurant. You can eat breakfast there all day! People love pancakes, bacon, and eggs. The waitress usually brings you a glass of ice water for free.

🗣️ Everyday Dialogue

Waiter: Hi guys, are you ready to order?

Customer: Yes, I'll have the cheeseburger and french fries, please.

Waiter: Would you like something to drink?

Customer: Just a glass of water with ice. Thank you!

📝 Grammar

Countable & Uncountable Nouns — Some / Any

Countable: an apple, two eggs, three tomatoes
Uncountable: water, rice, bread, milk, cheese

Any (negative & questions): Is there any bread? There isn't any juice.
A / An: I want an apple. She eats a sandwich.
How much / How many: How much water? How many eggs?

Staying Fit in America

📖 American Culture Notes

Staying active is very popular in the United States. Many Americans go jogging in the park or exercise at the gym. However, busy lifestyles can cause stress and health issues. When people feel sick, they should rest, drink water, or see a doctor for a prescription.

🗣️ Everyday Dialogue

Doctor: Hello. What seems to be the problem today?

Patient: I feel terrible. I have a bad headache, a cough, and a sore throat.

Doctor: Let me check... You have a fever. You should rest and take this medicine.

Patient: Should I go to work tomorrow?

Doctor: No, you shouldn't go to work. You must rest at home.

📝 Grammar

Modal Verb: Should / Shouldn't for Advice

Affirmative (+): Subject + should + verb (base form) — used to give good advice.
You should sleep 8 hours. She should see a doctor.
Negative (-): Subject + should not (shouldn't) + verb (base form) — used to advise against something.
You shouldn't drink cold water. He shouldn't go to work.
Questions (?): Should + subject + verb (base form)?
Should I take this medicine? Yes, you should. / No, you shouldn't.

Four Seasons in New England

📖 American Culture Notes

The northeast of the United States has four distinct seasons. Fall has beautiful red and gold leaves. Winter is very cold and snowy. Spring brings green trees, rain, and fresh flowers. Summer is hot and sunny, and many families go to the beach to swim and sunbathe.

School Rules in the USA

📖 American Culture Notes

American schools have clear rules to keep students safe. Students must arrive on time for classes. They must respect their teachers and classmates. In most public schools, students don't have to wear uniforms, but they must follow a dress code.

🗣️ Everyday Dialogue

Teacher: Remember class, you must turn off your mobile phones during the test.

Student: Can we use our dictionaries?

Teacher: No, you mustn't use dictionaries. It is against the rules.

Student: Do we have to write in ink?

Teacher: No, you don't have to write in ink. You can use a pencil.

📚 Vocabulary

Rules and Obligations

Must
To be required to do something by rule
"You must wear a seatbelt in the car."
Mustn't
To be prohibited from doing something
"You mustn't smoke in the hospital."
Have to
To be necessary to do due to circumstances
"I have to wash the dishes tonight."
Don't have to
Not necessary, optional
"We don't have to work on Sunday."
Arrive on time
To arrive at the correct scheduled time
"You must arrive on time for school."
Wear a uniform
To wear matching clothing required by school/work
"In some schools, they have to wear uniforms."
📝 Grammar

Obligation & Prohibition: Must, Mustn't, Have to, Don't have to

Must / Have to: Used for strong obligations and rules.
You must stop at a red light. I have to study for my test.
Mustn't: Used for prohibitions (things that are not allowed).
You mustn't park here. Students mustn't talk during the exam.
Don't have to: Used for things that are NOT necessary (optional).
Tomorrow is a holiday, so we don't have to get up early.

City Life vs. Country Life

📖 American Culture Notes

In the United States, lifestyles vary. Some Americans prefer living in big cities like New York because it is faster and more exciting. Others prefer quiet country towns. Country life is usually cheaper, safer, and cleaner, but it can be much slower and less convenient.

🗣️ Everyday Dialogue

John: Which city do you prefer, Boston or Chicago?

David: I prefer Boston. It is smaller and cleaner than Chicago.

John: Yes, but Chicago is more exciting and has better museums!

David: True, but Chicago is also much more expensive and noisier.

📝 Grammar

Comparative Adjectives

Short Adjectives: Add -er (e.g. big → bigger, cheap → cheaper, dry → drier). Use 'than' to compare.
A car is faster than a bicycle. The library is quieter than the cafe.
Long Adjectives: Use 'more' + adjective (e.g. expensive → more expensive, beautiful → more beautiful).
This watch is more expensive than that phone.
Irregular Comparatives: good → better, bad → worse, far → farther/further.
Fruits are better for you than sweets.

Long Adjectives: Use 'the most' + adjective (e.g. popular → the most popular).

📖 American Culture Notes

Technology is changing our world rapidly. Experts predict that cities in the future will be smart and clean. We will use electric self-driving vehicles, and robots will perform dangerous tasks. We won't use fossil fuels, which will make the air cleaner.

Negative (-): Subject + will not (won't) + verb.

Questions (?): Will + subject + verb?
Will robots speak English? Yes, they will. / No, they won't.

Mental Wellness in America

📖 American Culture Notes

Talking about emotions is very common in the United States. Many Americans share their feelings openly with friends, family, or counselors. People often practice exercises, jog, or walk in nature because it helps them feel happy and reduces anxiety.

🗣️ Everyday Dialogue

Jessica: Thanks, Sam. I feel a bit better now.

📚 Vocabulary

Feelings and Emotions

Feeling or showing strong annoyance or hostility
"The teacher was angry with the class."
📝 Grammar

Conjunctions: Because & So

Because: Used to give a reason.
I went to bed early because I was tired. She is happy because she passed.
So: Used to express a result.
I was tired, so I went to bed early. She passed the test, so she is happy.
Contrast: 'Because' introduces the cause; 'So' introduces the effect.

Zero Conditional (General Truths)

Use the Zero Conditional to talk about facts, scientific laws, or general truths (things that always happen under a certain condition).

You can also use 'when' instead of 'if': When you charge your phone, the battery icon appears.
